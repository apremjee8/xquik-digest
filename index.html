A kid from Shantou with no programming background got into Tsinghua University's computer science department by winning a high school informatics olympiad, then quit a job offer from Google Brain to start an AI company in Beijing with two of his college bandmates. Four years later that company released a 1 trillion parameter open-source AI model that outperformed every American closed model on coding benchmarks and became the fastest Chinese tech company in history to hit a $10 billion valuation.

His name is Yang Zhilin. The company is called Moonshot AI. The model is called Kimi K2.

Here is the story.

Yang was born in 1992 in Shantou, a small city in Guangdong province. In high school he had never written a line of code. He got selected for an informatics olympiad training program anyway. He won first prize at the Guangdong provincial level, which got him guaranteed admission to Tsinghua University.

He scored 667 on the gaokao, far above the Tsinghua cutoff. But the system placed him in Thermal Energy Engineering. He transferred to Computer Science in his sophomore year. He graduated in 2015 ranked first in his department class. During undergrad he was advised by Tang Jie, a Tsinghua professor who would later co-found another Chinese AI giant called Zhipu.

He went to Carnegie Mellon for his PhD under Ruslan Salakhutdinov and William Cohen. He finished in under four years. During that time he co-authored two of the most influential papers in modern AI, Transformer-XL and XLNet, which together shaped the long-context capabilities every modern LLM relies on. He worked at Facebook AI Research and Google Brain. He contributed to the original Google Gemini and Bard projects.

Then in November 2022 ChatGPT launched.

Yang flew back to the United States, looked at what OpenAI had done, and made up his mind. He told an interviewer later that he sensed two things were about to move at once, capital and talent, and when those two move together it is the rare moment when you can build a company from zero to one whose only purpose is AGI.

In March 2023 he founded Moonshot AI in Beijing with two Tsinghua classmates, Zhou Xinyu and Wu Yuxin. The three of them had been bandmates in a college rock group called Splay. The company name is a tribute to Pink Floyd's Dark Side of the Moon, Yang's favorite album. The company launched on the album's 50th anniversary.

He raised $60 million and built a 40-person team in three months. By 2024 he had raised over a billion dollars. The investors included Alibaba, Tencent, and Sequoia China. Moonshot AI became the fastest Chinese startup in history to reach a $10 billion valuation. ByteDance took four years. Pinduoduo took three. Moonshot did it in two.

In October 2023 they launched Kimi, a consumer chatbot with a 200,000 character context window, the longest in the world at the time. By 2024 it was running on Chinese hardware and had tens of millions of users.

Then on July 11, 2025 they released Kimi K2.

K2 is a 1 trillion parameter Mixture of Experts model that activates 32 billion parameters per inference. It is open weights. It beat GPT-4 and Claude on coding benchmarks. It outperformed DeepSeek V3 on agent tasks and tool use. Former OpenAI researcher Andrew Carr publicly said K2 communicates differently than other models, refusing to be sycophantic and pushing back on bad ideas the way few models do.

By early 2026 Moonshot had crossed $240 million in revenue. The Kimi K2.5 release exceeded the entire 2025 revenue total in under 20 days. K2.6 dropped in April 2026 with a SWE-Bench Pro score of 58.6, ahead of the leading closed-weight coding models at the time.

A kid from Shantou who had never coded a line in high school just released the open-source model that competes head-on with everything OpenAI, Google, and Anthropic have shipped.

He named it after the dark side of the moon.

Score: 83@qwerty_954792

SpaceX is moving into AI infrastructure, and today that is a terrestrial business: data centers on the ground, the same as it is for every other hyperscaler. What sets it apart is the bet layered on top, putting compute into orbit. That field is still thin. Google is probing it with Project Suncatcher, the startup Starcloud has trained a small model in space, and ventures backed by Bezos and Eric Schmidt are circling the idea, but most of it is early, closer to R&D hedge bets than committed strategy. SpaceX is the outlier, by far the largest and most aggressive player pitching a real pivot to space-based data centers. And it holds the decisive advantage in getting there: it already flies Falcon 9 and Falcon Heavy at a cost and cadence no rival comes close to, and it is the only one developing a vehicle, Starship, at the payload scale this actually requires.

Start on the ground, because that is where the money is today. xAI merged into SpaceX in February in an all-stock deal valuing the combined company at around 1.25 trillion dollars, so Colossus, X, and Grok are SpaceX assets, not adjacent Musk ventures. On the back of that, SpaceX has quietly turned into a compute landlord. In May, Anthropic agreed to rent the entire Colossus 1 data center in Memphis, more than 300 megawatts across 220,000 Nvidia GPUs, for 1.25 billion dollars a month running toward 2029. A regulatory filing this week added a second tenant: Google will pay 920 million dollars a month for roughly 110,000 GPUs from October into 2029, framed as bridge capacity for surging Gemini Enterprise demand. Between the two, SpaceX is now booking around 2.17 billion dollars a month in compute revenue, roughly 26 billion annualized, going into an IPO that begins trading on June 12 at a reported 1.75 trillion dollar valuation.

That is a real hyperscaler revenue line, and it cuts both ways. The disciplined read is that SpaceX is monetizing capacity it is not fully using and letting customers help fund its AI buildout before the listing. SpaceX described the Anthropic deal in exactly those terms, and Musk structured both leases as short-term with mutual cancellation at SpaceX's own request, which keeps the option to pull the capacity back for its own needs. The other read is that this is happening while xAI is straining to catch up at the frontier, and Musk is not hiding it. He is pushing Grok hard, announcing in late May that Grok V9-Medium, a 1.5-trillion-parameter model three times the size of the current production model, had finished training for a mid-June release, and xAI committed up to 60 billion dollars to acquire Cursor, or 10 billion for collaboration, to feed real coding data into Grok. But the gap is real and Musk concedes it. On the SWE-bench Verified coding benchmark, Claude Opus 4.7 sits near 87.6 percent while Grok 4 lands around 75, Grok holds only single-digit enterprise adoption, and Bloomberg reported that even some of SpaceX's own engineers reach for Claude over Grok for technical work. So the leases are both a disciplined landlord move and a quiet signal that the in-house model is not yet winning. Both are true.

Then there is Terafab, the vertical integration play across SpaceX, Tesla, and Intel. The ambition is staggering, one terawatt of compute per year, which Musk claims is roughly fifty times the combined output of every advanced chip line on Earth. SpaceX has filed for a fab in Grimes County, Texas, a first phase near 55 billion dollars with potential expansion to 119 billion, and Intel, which joined in April, brings its leading-edge 14A process and EMIB packaging. The honest read, again from SpaceX's own S-1, is that the arrangement is still largely non-binding, and chip analysts treat it less as a new standalone fab than as an Intel capacity expansion with Tesla and SpaceX as anchor customers.

The real bottleneck sits in the Netherlands. You cannot make leading-edge chips at this scale without ASML EUV lithography, and ASML is the only company on Earth that builds those machines. Its order book is already full with TSMC, Samsung, SK Hynix, Micron, and Intel. Fouquet has confirmed he has spoken with Musk and called him very serious, but his read was about capacity rather than endorsement: a project on this scale would stretch an already strained industry for years, and he expects the chip market to stay supply-constrained for the foreseeable future. There is no deal for direct orders. Musk is scheduled to make his case to ASML's engineers directly, in a virtual fireside chat with Fouquet at the company's internal technology conference the day before SpaceX starts trading. There is no alternative route here. EUV is too complex to reinvent, and assuming Terafab finds another path is not realistic.

Starship is the critical dependency, and the right way to look at it is the trajectory, not any single flight. The V3 vehicle and Raptor 3 engines flew for the first time in May, the upper stage came through a controlled reentry, and the heat shield held up reasonably well, all genuinely encouraging. The harder part is still ahead. Full reusability remains unproven, and without a Starship catch yet attempted, even SpaceX likely does not know precisely where that stands. The real question is not whether Starship can reach orbit; it can, and SpaceX keeps it suborbital for now because an immature vehicle is far safer on a path that ends in a guaranteed ocean splashdown. The question is whether SpaceX can make it reliable enough to commit to orbit and then fly ships and boosters back, catch them, and reuse them on a cadence of weeks rather than months. That capability is what makes cost to orbit collapse, and everything downstream waits on it. Significant engineering challenges remain, but the direction is clear and the progress is real.

Starlink is the proof that the manufacturing and deployment muscle already exists. The network crossed 12 million active customers on June 4, across more than 160 countries, up from 10 million in February, adding close to 28,000 customers a day. It now runs on more than 10,400 satellites, the largest constellation ever flown and roughly two-thirds of all active satellites in orbit, with regulatory headroom to reach 15,000. That is a company already building, launching, and operating hardware at industrial cadence, which is the exact capability the orbital data centers will demand, and Starlink is also the connectivity layer those orbital nodes would route through. The third-generation satellites coming this year, with far higher capacity, need Starship to deploy at scale, the same gating item as everything else.

The orbital data centers, which SpaceX calls AI satellites, are the payoff, and this is where the numbers need a cold eye. The January FCC filing requests authorization for up to a million satellites. SpaceX's own math runs from there: at 100 kilowatts of compute per ton, launching a million tons a year would add 100 gigawatts of annual capacity, with a stated path to a terawatt a year. Those are SpaceX's figures, and they are a very long way from physical reality. A million tons to orbit a year is, at roughly 100 tons per Starship flight, on the order of 10,000 launches a year, about 27 every single day. The entire world managed around 260 orbital launches in 2024, a record. The terawatt number is another order of magnitude on top of that. Even reaching 100 gigawatts launched in a single year by 2040 is hard to reconcile with any launch cadence the numbers support. Treat these as the ceiling of the vision, not a forecast.

The physics, though, is a real argument: continuous solar power, heat radiated straight into space, no grid interconnect, no water, no permitting fight. In the analysis about this scenario, I point to a more realistic arc, and even that looks demanding: a first test deployment around 2028, maybe 500 megawatts to 1 gigawatt a year by 2030, then a scaling phase toward 3 to 5 gigawatts a year by 2035. The launch math shows why even that is hard. At 100 tons to orbit per flight and 100 kilowatts per ton, you need about 100 Starship launches per gigawatt, so 3 to 5 gigawatts a year would mean 300 to 500 launches a year for the data centers alone, on top of everything else SpaceX flies. That is the modest end of the range, and it still implies a cadence well beyond anything achieved today. Anthropic has also expressed interest in developing multiple gigawatts of orbital compute with SpaceX, an early demand signal for the off-planet business, not just the terrestrial one.

None of this is the only iron in the fire, and the fallback routes are real. A slower Starship means leaning harder on terrestrial data centers, which the Colossus leases show is already a business. No Terafab means other foundries cover at least part of the chip demand. Starlink is operational and scaling, its current satellites do not need Starship, and only the much larger third generation does, where reusable Starship also drives down cost per kilogram to orbit.

That last point is the whole (scenario) thesis. The orbital-compute field either buys its launches or is years behind building them. SpaceX already flies its own, cheaper and more often than anyone, and is the only one developing a rocket at the scale this build-out needs. If the vertical integration closes from launch vehicle to fab to satellite, that cost-to-orbit advantage compounds in a way the rest of the field cannot match. The road is long and every layer can slip. But the terrestrial flywheel is already turning, two compute leases and 12 million Starlink customers are funding the bet, and if the orbital piece lands (High risk, High reward) the prize is enormous.

As you likely know by now (if you've been following my feed), yesterday, Canada dropped its massive "AI for All" National Strategy. It’s a multi-year, multi-billion-dollar bet on innovation, trust, and productivity.

But how does it stack up for AI in healthcare, medtech, clinicians, other healthcare professionals, and most importantly, patient care for 42 million of us?

🔑 Here are my five key takeaways ...

→ The "AI Missions" Era Begins 🏥

The strategy officially launches Canada's first-ever AI Missions (aka: mini-moohshots) program, backed by an initial $200M flagship health mission. The goal isn't just funding lab research; it’s about accelerating the deployment of mature and validated clinical AI reasoning systems into frontline patient care, chronic disease management, and slashing ER wait times.

→ Tackling the maladies of "pyjama time" & EHR-itis 🩺

A massive win for clinician wellness: the strategy explicitly prioritizes using AI to reduce administrative burdens. Expect accelerated funding and institutional backing for clinical tools like AI scribes to fight physician burnout and get doctors back to what matters: patients ... you, me, our families, neighbours, and friends.

→ Scaling "Sovereign Health Data" platforms 🔐

The strategy treats health data as a strategic national asset, and it is! It prioritizes building secure, Canadian-controlled data infrastructure. For health tech, this means a major push toward unified, high-quality medical datasets (bilingual and indigenous considerations included) with ironclad FPT privacy guardrails to protect patient privacy and grow trust.

→ Balancing Innovation vs Guardrails ⚖️

The Prime Minister framed the entire strategy around public trust. While the plan avoids immediate, heavy-handed AI-specific regulation (opting to rely on modernizing existing privacy and online safety laws), hello CMA and others, are already demanding strict clinical safety guardrails. This area of follow-on legislation and its sequencing (where the Trudeau government failed) is critical.

→ Elevating Canada to be an "AI Skills Nation" ... eh! 🎓

It’s not just about algorithms; it’s about the people using them. The plan introduces free, accessible AI training nationwide and offers specialized AI tools/agents to post-secondary students ... including those in medicine.

The BIG goal is to build an inherently AI-literate national, and more specifically, healthcare-implicated, workforce.

📍 And it is here, on this national issue of foundational AI literacy and capacity (with many details still to come), where this strategy definitely 'meets the moment.'

Bottom line: I'm ALL for AI for ALL ✅

Most doctors interact with AI through their EHR. The problem with that is EHRs aren't harnesses and when doctors use them they can't fine-tune the context, the model, or the performance. Instead, they're stuck with someone else's configuration. So if you're using AI in your medical practice, you 100% need to be managing and customizing these 5 things.

1. Deployment
2. Token optimization
3. Model usage and routing
4. Context engineering
5. Agent handoff orchestration

1. Deployment
- This used to be your EHR vendor's job. You paid them for the software, they deployed and maintained it under HIPAA. But now you're the builder, and with agent runtime (how agents command the code), you absolutely DO NOT want to hand that access back to a vendor when you're the one paying the LLM API bill. The only questions you should be asking are whether the deployment is HIPAA-compliant and whether you have audit trails and API session logging.

2. Token optimization
- You're burning through tokens before a project even starts, sometimes 100k or more. You should know every file and every system prompt your agent uses. Otherwise you're just lighting money on fire.

3. Model usage and routing
- Not all doctors are the same, but they're all doctors. They specialize in the medicine they practice, and LLMs are no different. They're all LLMs, but they don't all have the same training. If Claude Opus 4.8 is an Ortho, and the only model your EHR pushes is Claude Opus 4.8, then every clinical case and every operational task in your practice is being managed by an Ortho. That's why you need benchmarks: to understand which model performs best for which task and which agent.

4. Context engineering
- This is the brain of your entire practice: your clinical operations, your SOPs, your panel, your staff, your payments, your skills. If you can't customize this, you're using bolt-on AI, your data is NOT YOUR DATA, and you do not OWN YOUR PRACTICE.

5. Agent handoff orchestration
- Agent-to-agent communication is what makes AI special. It lets you run multiple agents in parallel (more than one agent running and completing tasks at the same time), with the right models, the right skills, and the least token consumption. Where practices go wrong is treating this like a Ronco Showtime Rotisserie. You can't set it and forget it. Agents need management just like people do, so if you aren't the one setting the rules, you're not really the boss. Your EHR vendor is.

This is why every doctor and clinician should be practicing with a clinical agentic harness like Cline, not a legacy EHR with architecture designed for pre-AI era deployment processes.

To be clear, my concern isn't the mechanics of a reverse split alone.

The Company reported positive net income, no meaningful debt, approximately $88 million in cash, substantial digital asset holdings, and what appears to be a significantly stronger balance sheet than many shareholders have seen in prior years. At the same time, shareholders are being asked to authorize the Board to implement a share consolidation of up to 12:1 at its sole discretion without returning for further shareholder approval.

Reasonable shareholders can support Nasdaq compliance, institutional expansion, UCITS initiatives, AMCs, Brazilian market development, and broader international growth while still asking a simple question:

Why is a reverse split the preferred path relative to other available alternatives?

The issue isn't whether a reverse split can raise the nominal share price. It can.

The issue is whether shareholders have been given enough information to understand why this approach is being prioritized over other options that may exist given the Company's balance sheet, asset base, and stated growth initiatives.

For example:

Share repurchases

Asset-backed credit facilities

Strategic financing alternatives

Other capital allocation strategies designed to support shareholder value while maintaining listing compliance

Perhaps management has evaluated these alternatives and concluded they are less attractive.

If so, additional transparency surrounding that analysis would be helpful.

A reverse split does not create intrinsic value by itself. Its purpose is largely structural - supporting exchange compliance, institutional eligibility, and market access.

If that is the rationale, shareholders should understand how management expects those benefits to translate into measurable long-term shareholder outcomes.

Equally important is the level of discretion being requested.

Shareholders are not being asked to approve a fixed consolidation ratio. They are being asked to authorize a consolidation of up to 12:1, at a time and ratio determined solely by the Board, without returning for further shareholder approval.

That is a meaningful grant of authority.

The concern is that when shareholders are asked to grant significant discretionary authority while the company trades at a valuation many believe does not reflect its underlying assets, operations, and future potential, shareholders naturally begin asking who ultimately benefits if that valuation disconnect persists.

The issue also extends beyond the reverse split itself.

The issue is not whether management has worked hard or achieved operational milestones.

The issue is whether compensation growth remains appropriately aligned with shareholder outcomes.

When shareholders have experienced significant equity destruction over multiple years, compensation increases become difficult to evaluate without a clear demonstration of how those increases correspond to measurable value creation for shareholders.

That is why many shareholders become frustrated.

Most long-term shareholders understand that building a business takes time. They understand markets move in cycles. They understand difficult periods occur and that management teams often make sacrifices along the way.

What becomes difficult to reconcile is when shareholders are asked to continue accepting dilution, declining share prices, expanded discretionary authority, and increasing executive compensation, while many of the same shareholders remain deeply underwater on their investments.

The question is not whether management deserves to be compensated. The question is whether shareholders are seeing a level of value creation that justifies the compensation being awarded.

Alignment matters. Accountability is not opposition. It is ownership.

The broader question is whether management has sufficiently articulated the roadmap that follows it, and whether shareholders have been provided enough information to evaluate why this path is preferable to other available alternatives.

Shareholders can support the business, support growth, support Nasdaq compliance, and still expect a clear explanation of how major governance and capital allocation decisions are expected to create value for the owners of the company.

And if the market continues assigning a value that many shareholders believe does not adequately reflect the operating business, international distribution network, ETP platform, institutional relationships, and balance sheet, one eventually begins to wonder whether the better investment is simply owning shares, owning more shares, or perhaps acquiring the company itself.

I disagree that we have no way to value crypto projects.

Is it as straightforward as valuing a company based on revenue, cash flow, and earnings? No. But that doesn't mean we're flying blind.

The first question should always be: What real-world problem does this project solve?

Does it reduce friction? Increase efficiency? Improve interoperability? Lower costs? Move value faster? Secure data? Enable new markets? If the answer is yes, then we're already moving beyond pure speculation and into utility.

Retail gets confused because too many people focus on token supply, logos, influencers, and price action instead of following where actual value and capital may flow in the future.

I look at RWU, adoption, trust, partnerships, network effects, regulatory positioning, and whether the technology solves a problem large institutions are willing to pay for.

Take XRP. The bull case isn't "number go up." It's global liquidity, cross-border settlement, tokenization, stablecoins, custody, and a decade of relationships Ripple has built with financial institutions around the world.

Take XLM. It's focused on efficient value transfer, remittances, financial inclusion, and connecting traditional finance with digital assets.

Take QNT. Its value proposition is interoperability. If the future contains thousands of blockchains, banking systems, CBDCs, tokenized assets, and private ledgers, somebody has to connect them.

Take Zebec. The thesis is programmable money, real-time payroll, streaming payments, and modernizing how money moves between employers, employees, consumers, and businesses.

None of these projects are valuable because they have a cool logo or a low token supply. They're valuable if they become part of the infrastructure that future digital finance runs on.

That's how I think about crypto.

Not "How many coins are there?"

But:

"Will trillions of dollars of value eventually flow through this network, use this technology, or depend on this infrastructure?"

That's the question that matters.

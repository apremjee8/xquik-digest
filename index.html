Signal Score: 233 @withcline

Most doctors interact with AI through their EHR. The problem with that is EHRs aren't harnesses and when doctors use them they can't fine-tune the context, the model, or the performance. Instead, they're stuck with someone else's configuration. So if you're using AI in your medical practice, you 100% need to be managing and customizing these 5 things.

1. Deployment
2. Token optimization
3. Model usage and routing
4. Context engineering
5. Agent handoff orchestration

1. Deployment
- This used to be your EHR vendor's job. You paid them for the software, they deployed and maintained it under HIPAA. But now you're the builder, and with agent runtime (how agents command the code), you absolutely DO NOT want to hand that access back to a vendor when you're the one paying the LLM API bill. The only questions you should be asking are whether the deployment is HIPAA-compliant and whether you have audit trails and API session logging.

2. Token optimization
- You're burning through tokens before a project even starts, sometimes 100k or more. You should know every file and every system prompt your agent uses. Otherwise you're just lighting money on fire.

3. Model usage and routing
- Not all doctors are the same, but they're all doctors. They specialize in the medicine they practice, and LLMs are no different. They're all LLMs, but they don't all have the same training. If Claude Opus 4.8 is an Ortho, and the only model your EHR pushes is Claude Opus 4.8, then every clinical case and every operational task in your practice is being managed by an Ortho. That's why you need benchmarks: to understand which model performs best for which task and which agent.

4. Context engineering
- This is the brain of your entire practice: your clinical operations, your SOPs, your panel, your staff, your payments, your skills. If you can't customize this, you're using bolt-on AI, your data is NOT YOUR DATA, and you do not OWN YOUR PRACTICE.

5. Agent handoff orchestration
- Agent-to-agent communication is what makes AI special. It lets you run multiple agents in parallel (more than one agent running and completing tasks at the same time), with the right models, the right skills, and the least token consumption. Where practices go wrong is treating this like a Ronco Showtime Rotisserie. You can't set it and forget it. Agents need management just like people do, so if you aren't the one setting the rules, you're not really the boss. Your EHR vendor is.

This is why every doctor and clinician should be practicing with a clinical agentic harness like Cline, not a legacy EHR with architecture designed for pre-AI era deployment processes.

Signal Score: 200 @AIForDoctors

One of the least visible challenges in healthcare AI is data ingestion.

Yet it is often where the hardest engineering problems live.

Before a model can make a prediction, healthcare data must first be collected, transported, standardized, validated, and stored.

That sounds straightforward.

Healthcare makes it remarkably difficult.

Consider a single hospitalized patient.

Data may originate from:

EHR systems

ICU telemetry monitors

laboratory information systems

pharmacy databases

radiology platforms

wearable devices

clinician documentation

Each system often uses different formats, standards, and workflows.

This creates a fragmentation problem.

Healthcare organizations commonly work with standards such as:

HL7

HL7 FHIR

DICOM

LOINC

SNOMED CT

But real-world environments frequently contain legacy systems, proprietary interfaces, and local customizations.

Interoperability remains one of the biggest barriers to scalable healthcare AI.

Data quality introduces another challenge.

Clinical data often includes:

missing values

duplicate entries

timestamp inconsistencies

unit mismatches

documentation errors

If these issues enter the pipeline unchecked, every downstream analytic system inherits the problem.

Real-time monitoring adds even more complexity.

An ICU bed may continuously generate:

ECG waveforms

arterial pressure traces

SpO₂ streams

ventilator telemetry

infusion pump events

Processing these streams requires low-latency ingestion architectures capable of handling thousands of events per second.

Scalability quickly becomes a distributed systems challenge.

This is why modern healthcare AI platforms increasingly depend on:

Apache Kafka event streaming

HL7 FHIR APIs

ETL orchestration

schema validation

stream processing engines

observability infrastructure

The deeper insight is this:

Healthcare data is not difficult because there is too little of it.

Healthcare data is difficult because it is produced by many independent systems that were never originally designed to work together.

And that may explain why infrastructure problems frequently limit healthcare AI more than model performance.

In your experience, what creates the bigger obstacle for healthcare AI adoption: data interoperability or data quality?

Why is a reverse split the preferred path relative to other available alternatives?

The issue is whether shareholders have been given enough information to understand why this approach is being prioritized over other options that may exist given the Company's balance sheet, asset base, and stated growth initiatives.

For example:

Share repurchases

Asset-backed credit facilities

Strategic financing alternatives

Other capital allocation strategies designed to support shareholder value while maintaining listing compliance

Perhaps management has evaluated these alternatives and concluded they are less attractive.

Equally important is the level of discretion being requested.

Shareholders are not being asked to approve a fixed consolidation ratio. They are being asked to authorize a consolidation of up to 12:1, at a time and ratio determined solely by the Board, without returning for further shareholder approval.

That is a meaningful grant of authority.

The issue also extends beyond the reverse split itself.
